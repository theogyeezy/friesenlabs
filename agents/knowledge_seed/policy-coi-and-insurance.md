# Policy: COI and insurance

Most commercial and institutional customers require a certificate of insurance (COI) before
work begins, and many require being named as **additional insured** on the policy. Handling
this early keeps it from becoming a last-minute deal-killer.

**When it comes up.** Property-management firms, REITs, healthcare facilities, and municipal
buyers almost always require a COI naming them as additional insured before they'll sign,
especially on capital projects. Boards frequently make the COI a condition of approval.

**How to handle it.** The moment a customer signals a COI requirement, request it from our
broker and copy the customer so they can see it's in motion before their decision date. Don't
wait for the formal ask — on a board-approved retrofit, an outstanding COI can hold up a vote.

**What to put in the record.** Log the COI request as an open item on the deal with the
customer's exact requirement (who must be named, any limits specified) and the broker turnaround.
A retrofit "blocked on COI + board approval" is a clear, answerable status; "waiting on
paperwork" is not.

**Vendor packet.** The COI usually travels with the rest of the vendor packet — W-9, references,
and any procurement onboarding forms. Submit the whole packet together to avoid round-trips.
